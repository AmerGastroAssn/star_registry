Welcome to the first AGA STAR Registry Newsletter! The STAR Registry team is thankful for your support of this important initiative and hopes to keep in touch with you via these newsletters, which will provide you with insight from other sites, answers to frequently asked questions, links to resources, enrollment metrics, and more.

AGA is looking to the future and wants to ensure that GIs have innovative tools to care for their patients. With your support, we are collecting critical data to support this process and bring new technology to practice in a safe and timely manner.

Here are the goals of AGA’s Registry initiative:

  1. As a neutral, objective broker, AGA will establish observational research registries to help companies gather the data needed by payors, purchasers, risk-bearing organization and regulatory agencies to support the approval, coverage, reimbursement and adoption of new technologies, therapies and procedures.
  2. Develop observational research registries to help bring new medical devices to gastroenterologists and their patients.
  3. Provide data to allow stakeholders to evaluate the efficacy of a device and document safety and durability.

Thank you for helping to make this possible. If you have any questions, please reach out to a member of the AGA registry team:

A Message from Ashish Atreja, MD, MPH, chair of the AGA Registry Oversight Subcommittee
“The announcement of the AGA STAR Registry in March 2014 was a huge milestone for the AGA Center for GI Innovation and Technology. With this registry, we’re setting new standards for comparative evaluation of new technologies in the GI space. The STAR Registry will be the first registry to compare Laparoscopic Nissen Fundoplication (LNF) surgery versus Transoral Incisionless Fundoplication (TIF®). The goal is to follow 500 patients over three years to gather a critical source of long-term data to compare the safety, effectiveness and side-effect profile of these two procedures. This real-world data will advance the treatment of GERD patients, and we are grateful for your participation in this important initiative.”

World Changers Shaped Here
Teams’ big ideas win big grants
Four teams of entrepreneurial students working on innovative solutions to a range of real-world issues pitched their proposals and received $5,000 startup grants at the Big iDeas Business Plan Competition. The event capped Big iDeas Demo Day, the annual student project showcase.
DSO to perform student’s composition
First-year student Chase Dobson ’18 began composing at age 11, and his works have been heard nationally on the radio program From the Top. Next week his Piano Concerto No. 1 will be played by Meadows faculty member and international concert artist Lucille Chung during its world premiere by the Dallas Symphony Orchestra.
Responsibility on the cyber battlefield
How does the existing legal framework apply to cyber warfare? Thanks to a Fulbright Scholars Grant, SMU Dedman School of Law Assistant Professor Chris Jenks has begun a six-month research project in Melbourne, Australia, where he is studying how emerging technologies impact accountability in armed conflict.
Monique Achu's inspiring school
Since an online fundraising campaign for the Brooklyn middle school where she works went viral, Monique Achu ’02 has been deluged with calls and emails from media and supporters. Achu says, “I wasn’t sure how I would use it, but now I’m really glad I took a PR class” at SMU.
Community, belonging and Meadows
Molly Beach Murphy ’09 recently returned to the Hilltop to direct The Sparrow, a play written by SMU theatre alumni Nathan Allen ’00, Chris Mathews ’02 and Jake Minton ’02. She calls the play “a big story about community and belonging” and collaborating with Meadows “a true thrill and honor.”
Cox collaboration creates ‘Quant U’
AT&T employees David Daines ’12, ’13 and Lyndsey Aldridge ’02, ’07 worked with Cox School of Business faculty to present “Quant U,” a business analytics certification course for the new AT&T Partnership Exchange. Participants left with cutting-edge business skills and graduation day propeller beanies.
Bitcoin cons net at least $11 million
New cyber security research by SMU’s Marie Vasek and Tyler Moore finds that web-based scams involving Bitcoin – the digital world’s most popular virtual currency – have robbed 13,000 unsuspecting customers of at least $11 million. Vasek calls that estimate “on the low end.”
New tennis complex dedicated
The University dedicated its new 45,000-square-foot tennis complex February 20, opening a competition-level facility for practice, training and tournament play. Among the features are outdoor and indoor courts equipped with state-of-the-art technology. Check out the new home to SMU’s top-ranked tennis teams at upcoming men’s and women’s home games.
Political insiders weigh in on 2004
SMU’s Center for Presidential History’s Collective Memory Project, a nonpartisan oral history collaboration focusing on various presidential administrations, recently published its first installment online. Dick Cheney, Joe Trippi and other insiders share analysis and anecdotes on The Election of 2004, a new resource-rich website.
Celebrating historic levels of generosity
SMU begins the final year of its $1 billion Second Century Campaign with the momentum of $4.8 million in new commitments from alumni and other donors. The gifts will support undergraduate and graduate scholarships, annual awards for outstanding accounting faculty and a new educational leadership institute.
